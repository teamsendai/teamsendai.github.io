Experiment

AFM Observation

In order to evaluate our concept of Size-controllable Linear Homo-multimer (SLH), we first tried to confirm its shape by AFM. We prepared a sample of monomer which does not connect each other by adding single-strand loop of DNA to prevent blunt end stacking between the monomers. Also, we have samples which have and do not have the twist. Hereafter we call them like monomer(+/-), where the first sign corresponds to with and without stacking, and the next sign corresponds to with and without twist (Table 1).

Stack Twist
Monomer (+/+) + +
Monomer (+/-) + -
Monomer (-/+) - +
Monomer (-/-) - -

Firstly, the result of AFM imaging of monomer(-/-) is shown in Fig. 1. The topographical data shows that the monomer(-/-) is 8nm high, suggesting that it has at least 4 layers of DNA double helices, because the diameter of DNA double helix is 2 nm. The height also indicates that the monomer has a shaft inside the cylinder. Although they are expected to not stack, they form multimers. Perhaps this is because single-stranded loop at the ends were not long enough.

Secondly, we replaced some of the staples so that the monomer can stack by blunt end stacking. This monomer is called monomer(+/-). This sample does not have twist in the shaft, thus it is expected to stack as many as they can. The AFM image of this sample actually shows this (Fig. 2-a). A 10-mer assembly and its section profile for example are shown in Fig. 2-b and 2-c.

Thirdly, we observed the monomer (+/+), which is expected to have a limited number of stack caused by the twist of the shaft.

Fig. 3 shows its AFM image. b,c shows an AFM image and its section of a typical monomer (hexamer) of (+/+). Here, the number of stacking seems to be smaller compared to that of the monomer without twist (+/-).These results clearly shows that our mechanism to control the number of stacking actually works!

To investigate the effect of stacking and twist, we analyzed the stacking number of monomer (-/-), monomer(+/-), and monomer(+/+), then made histograms (Fig. 4). For each monomer type, the histogram was made from three different AFM images (row data are available at the Material & Method). This data shows that there were different distributions of stacking number in three monomers. Monomer(-/-) tended to exist only by a monomer, while the stacking number of monomer(+/-) was dispersed. As concerns monomer(+/+), most of them formed a dimer, where the longest multimer consisted of 8 monomers. This indicates that the stacking number of monomer(+/+) was limited by a twisted shaft.

Cryo-TEM (Cryo-transmission electron microscopy)

To assure the three-dimensional structure, we observed the monomer by cryo-TEM. Fig.5 is a comparison between the cross-section of the monomer in caDNAno design and cryo-TEM image. The cryo-TEM image was recovered from multiple raw data using principal component analysis (detail in Material & Method). The diameter of the hollow cylinder and the shaft were 18 nm and 5.7 nm in Fig.5-b, and 18 nm and 6.5 nm in Fig. 5-c. This size is in good agreement with the caDAno design. Therefore we concluded that the monomer structure is properly formed.

We observed extremely longer homomultimer in cryo-TEM compared with AFM, which may be because the force from AFM probe and adsorption to surface were not applied to cryo-EM samples. From these reasons, our DNA origami structure could make a long stacking structure correctly in the solution. In Fig. 6-a, monomer(+/-) seemed to connect each other unlimitedly, while there was a limitation in the length of the multimer observed in Fig. 6-b. Thus, this also suggests that monomer(+/+) can restrict the size of the multimer.

Analysis of Stacking

To identify the ability about stacking number control of our structure, we checked the stacking angle of 3 different sample (monomer(+/+) monomer(+/-) monomer(-/-)) in the AFM images. Each straight line shows the central part of DNA origami structure, and the angular deviation from the center is evaluated. In monomer(+/+), the number of stacking is small, where the average stacking angle is 1.13 ±2.1°. It shows that each monomers relatively connect straight within the limit of stacking number. In case of monomer(+/-), the monomers can connect unlimitedly. Observed structures are rather linear and long, and the average angle takes values in the same range. One typical sample has the angle in the first part (5 monomers) 1.9°±2.5 , in the second part (6 monomers) 1.2°±2.2°,and in the third part (8monomers) 1.7°±6.5°. In these monomer(+/+) and monomer(+/-), monomers bind each other in the average 2° or less. But, there are some mismatch connections in the longer structure of monomer(+/-). It may be difficult to make a longer structure. In the monomer(-/-), they combine with larger angular deviation compared to others. This is because the monomers do not connect perfectly because of mismatch at the shaft, but still the cylinders can stack.

Optimizing Staple Concentration

To optimize yield of complete monomers that have a shaft inside, we need to adjust the concentrations of M13(M), cylinder staple (C), and shaft staple (S). The yields of monomers were evaluated based on the height of the AFM image. When M:C:S=1:25:50, the yield was around 40% (Fig. 8-a). By contrast, he yields improved to 95%, for the ratio 1:10:50 (Fig. 8-b) which we used for the experiments.

Annealing Condition

We also need to tune the annealing profile (Fig. 9). We tried profiles of Fig. 9-a, 9-c first. By the profile of Fig. 9-a, the cylinder was formed but we could not find shafts, because the time of annealing was too short to form a three-dimensional shape (Fig. 9-d). By the profile in Fig. 9-c, there were also no monomers that formed as we desired. The overlong annealing seemed to induce unexpected DNA reactions.

Gel Electrophoresis

We conducted gel shift assay in order to evaluate the size of structures (Fig. 10). There was a clear difference in bands between DNA origami structures and M13mp18, which verified the construction of the structure. On the other hand, monomer(+/+) and (+/-) were supposed to form multimers, but we could not find major difference in the bands between monomer(+/+), (+/-) and monomer(-/+), (-/-). It means that the structures are too fragile to keep its assembly in the gel electrophoresis. The multimers may be caught in the mesh of gel and broken into smaller pieces.
