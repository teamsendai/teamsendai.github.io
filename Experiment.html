Experiment

AFM Observation

In order to create Size-controllable Linear Homomultimer (SLH), we firstly designed a monomer which don’t make stacking and observed it by using AFM (Fig.1).

In Fig. 1-c, the blue graph reaches the top in the middle of it. This indicates that the monomer has a shaft inside. To protect against stacking, we designed the staples, which consist of the edges of the monomer, 3nt shorter than that of stacking monomer. But in Fig. 1-a, the monomers unexpectedly stacked and formed a multimer. This indicates that the design was not suitable for existing only as a monomer. The monomers can be more stable when the staples at the edge of the monomer are much shorter or loosed.

Then, we conneted monomers and created linear homomultimers (Fig. 2).

In Fig. 2-a, monomers which has a non-twisted shaft connected a lot, and formed large size of multimers. Fig. 2-b, c indicates that the multimer consists of 10 monomers and have a shaft inside.

Next, we controlled the stacking number of monomers and observed the multimers under the same observation condition in Fig. 2 (Fig. 3).

Compared to Fig.2, the number of stacking was limited. Fig. 3-b, c indicates that the multimer consists of 6 monomers and has a shaft inside.

We analyzed the stacking number of monomer, homomultimer without twist, and SLH, then made a histogram and Poisson distribution, respectively (Fig. 4). Each stacking number was counted by 3 different images taken from AFM.

SLH were designed to connect 11 monomers, but most of them consisted of 2 to 4 monomers. According to Fig. 2-a, some monomers without twist created a large size of homomultimer, but the peak of the distribution almost resembled that of SLH. There are several reasons why the monomers didn’t create a long linear homomultimer. As the monomers make stacking, the entropy decreases. But it generally increases to stabilize the whole energy. Therefore, monomers cannot create a long multimer. It seems that the multimers which consists of about 3 or 4 monomers is the most stable structure, which is in the equilibrium of the energy. Moreover, the multimers might be broken when observing by AFM. The structures might be broken when the sample adsorbed onto mica. Also, the cantilever might break while attaching to them.　Furthermore, as the monomers which have a twisted shaft create SLH, the movement of the shaft is limited. This also decreases the possibility of stacking monomers.

The result of cryo-TEM (Cryo-electron microscopy)

Fig 5. shows a design of caDNAno2 image(cross section) and cryo-TEM result. In cryo-TEM image, the diameter of hollow cylinder and shaft are 24nm and 2.6nm. This size is clealy same with caDNAno2 desgin. Therefore, we concluded the monomer structure is correctly made.

In addition, Fig.6 indicates a linear homomultimer of cryo-TEM image. The width of hollow cylinder part is 18nm(Blue arrow) and shaft part(Red arrow) is 5nm. These value are also same with designed structure. Then, the linear homomultimer is correctly made by monomor stacks. And, some linear structures extremely connected longer than structures which observed in AFM. This difference is caused by the observing method. In AFM imaging, DNA origami structure is bonded to a mica suface via bivalent ions. During this process, some DNA structures which are making linear homomultimer are broken because of the bonding physical force. From these reason, Our DNA origami structure could make a long stacking structure correctly in the solution.

Gel electrophoresis

We conducted gel shift assay in order to confirm whether the structures were created as we desired (Fig. 8).

The bands appeared in the same position. It means that the structures were not created properly and formed unexpected shape. This might be caused because while conducting the gel shift assay, the multimers were caught on the mesh of the gel and broken into smaller pieces. This result suggests that the strength of stacking was not so strong and explains why the long size of homomultimer hardly exists in Fig. 4.

Monomer formation
Ratio of concentration

To investigate the good condition to produce monomers which have a shaft inside, we adjusted the concentrations of M13, cylinder staple (C), and shaft staple (S). The yields of monomers were calculated based on the height of the image taken by AFM. When the ratio of concentrations was M13:C:S=1:25:50, the yield was 50% (Fig. 9-a). By contrast, when the ratio was 1:10:50, the yields improved and reached to 80%, which suggests the high yields (Fig. 9-b).

Annealing condition

The annealing condition to create a monomer which has a shaft inside is showed in Fig. 10-2. Before we decided the condition, we tried the two other conditions described as Fig. 10-1 and 10-3. When the condition was Fig. 10-1, the cylinder formed a flat shape because the time of annealing was too short to form a three-dimensional shape (Fig. 10-d). On the other hand, when we annealed under the condition represented in Fig. 10-3, the monomer formed more complex shape than in Fig. 10-d (Fig. 10-e). But almost all of them were a flat shape as well as in Fig. 10-d. The gradient of annealing was so gentle that unexpected hybridizations might be induced. We picked up 20 monomers arbitrarily from Fig. 10-d and 10-e respectively, and calculated the yields of the monomer which is created as desired. There were no monomers as designed in both conditions.
